Hydrogen atom and the periodic table
Quantum shells and subshell capacities organize the elements

Guided reading

Hydrogenic quantum numbers provide the language of shells and subshells used for multi-electron atoms. Electron spin, the exclusion principle and empirical energy ordering turn this language into the block structure of the periodic table.

Subshell capacity

For fixed l there are \(2l+1\) values of m. Including two spin states per orbital gives

\[C_l=2(2l+1)\]

Shell capacity

Summing over l = 0 to n - 1 gives

\[C_n=2\sum_{l=0}^{n-1}(2l+1)=2n^2\]
Madelung ordering
  • Fill the subshell with the smaller n + l first.
  • If n + l is tied, fill the smaller n first.
  • This produces 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, ...
Periodic blocks

The s, p, d and f blocks have widths 2, 6, 10 and 14 because those are their electron capacities. Periods follow the order in which subshells are filled.

Madelung ordering is a useful empirical rule, not an exact theorem for every atom.

From orbital multiplicity to capacity

For a subshell l, the allowed m values give \(2l+1\) spatial orbitals. The Pauli principle permits two electrons of opposite spin in each orbital, producing capacity \(2(2l+1)\). Summing all subshells in a hydrogenic shell gives \(2n^2\), explaining the familiar shell capacities before electron-electron interactions modify the energy ordering.

Why the Madelung rule is needed

Hydrogen energy depends only on n, but in many-electron atoms screening and penetration make energy depend strongly on l. Low-l orbitals penetrate closer to the nucleus and often feel a larger effective charge. The empirical n + l rule summarizes the resulting order reasonably well. Exceptions occur because the true problem includes electron repulsion, exchange and relativistic effects, so the rule should guide rather than replace calculation.

Hydrogen is the starting vocabulary

In hydrogen, all l values within one n shell are degenerate. Electron-electron interactions in many-electron atoms break that degeneracy, making subshell filling nontrivial.
