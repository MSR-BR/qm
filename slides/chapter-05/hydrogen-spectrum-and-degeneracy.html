Hydrogen spectrum and degeneracy
The Coulomb energy depends only on the principal quantum number
7 / 15
Guided reading

Polynomial termination fixes the hydrogen energy. In the ideal nonrelativistic Coulomb problem, all states with the same principal quantum number share one energy even when their l and m values differ.

Energy spectrum
\[E_n=-\frac{E_0}{n^2}=-\frac{13.6\,\text{eV}}{n^2},\qquad n=1,2,3,\ldots\]

The continuum begins at E = 0; 13.6 eV is the ground-state ionization energy.

Allowed labels
\[l=0,1,\ldots,n-1,\qquad m=-l,\ldots,+l\]

A hydrogen state is specified by the triplet \((n,l,m)\) before spin is included.

Orbital degeneracy

For fixed n, count all m states in every allowed l subshell:

\[g_n=\sum_{l=0}^{n-1}(2l+1)=n^2\]

Including the two spin states doubles this to \(2n^2\).

Transitions and ionization
\[h\nu=|E_{n_f}-E_{n_i}|\]

Bound-bound transitions create discrete spectral lines; a final energy at or above zero corresponds to ionization.

Degeneracy signals extra symmetry

Spherical symmetry explains m degeneracy, but the additional l degeneracy of the Coulomb problem is stronger. Fine structure and external fields later lift parts of this ideal degeneracy.
