Chapter 4 · Item 4.8
Finite-well quantization and energy levels
Parity branches and graphical intersections select the allowed energies
8 / 15
Guided reading

Boundary matching produces transcendental equations rather than a simple integer formula. Their roots are the bound-state energies. A dimensionless graphical method makes the number and parity of the levels visible.

Quantization conditions

At \(x=a\), logarithmic-derivative matching gives

\[k\tan(ka)=\kappa\qquad\text{(even)}\]
\[-k\cot(ka)=\kappa\qquad\text{(odd)}\]
Dimensionless circle

Define \(z=ka\), \(y=\kappa a\) and \(z_0=a\sqrt{2mV_0}/\hbar\). Then

\[z^2+y^2=z_0^2\]

The allowed states are intersections of this quarter-circle with \(y=z\tan z\) or \(y=-z\cot z\).

Reading the graph
  • Each intersection corresponds to one bound energy.
  • Even and odd solutions alternate as energy increases.
  • Increasing \(V_0\), \(a\), or \(m\) increases \(z_0\) and usually adds levels.
  • In one dimension an attractive finite well supports at least one bound state.
Recover the energy
\[E=-\frac{\hbar^2\kappa^2}{2m}=-\frac{\hbar^2y^2}{2ma^2}\]

A shallow state has small \(\kappa\), a long exterior tail and energy close to zero. A deeply bound state has larger \(\kappa\) and stronger localization.

Root-finding workflow

Choose a parity branch, bracket a root between tangent or cotangent singularities, solve numerically, then reconstruct \(E\). Always check that \(-V_0<E<0\) and that the corresponding tail decays.
