Rectangular barrier and quantum tunneling
Evanescent waves carry amplitude through a classically forbidden region
13 / 15
Guided reading

For a rectangular barrier and \(0<E<V_0\), the wave inside is exponential rather than oscillatory. Both exponentials are needed in a finite barrier because the second boundary can reflect an evanescent component back toward the first.

Barrier setup
\[V(x)=\begin{cases}V_0,&0
\[k=\frac{\sqrt{2mE}}{\hbar},\qquad \kappa=\frac{\sqrt{2m(V_0-E)}}{\hbar}\]
Regional wave functions
\[\psi_I=Ae^{ikx}+Be^{-ikx}\]
\[\psi_{II}=Ce^{\kappa x}+De^{-\kappa x}\]
\[\psi_{III}=Fe^{ikx}\]
Exact tunneling probability
\[T=\left[1+\frac{V_0^2\sinh^2(\kappa L)}{4E(V_0-E)}\right]^{-1}\]

The result is nonzero for every finite \(L\) and finite \(V_0-E\): quantum mechanics permits transmission where classical mechanics predicts none.

Opaque-barrier limit

When \(\kappa L\gg1\), \(\sinh(\kappa L)\approx e^{\kappa L}/2\), so

\[T\approx\frac{16E(V_0-E)}{V_0^2}e^{-2\kappa L}\]

The exponential factor explains the extreme sensitivity of tunneling to width, mass and energy deficit.

Amplitude is not current

Inside the barrier the stationary evanescent components do not behave like independent traveling plane waves. Compute \(T\) from the asymptotic incident and transmitted currents after matching both boundaries.
