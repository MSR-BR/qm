Quantum harmonic oscillator (QHO): model and scaling
Parabolic confinement, natural scales and a dimensionless equation
2 / 15
Guided reading

The quantum harmonic oscillator (QHO) is the local model for any stable equilibrium: near a potential minimum, the leading nonconstant term is quadratic. The calculation becomes transparent after choosing the oscillator's natural length and energy scales.

From Hooke's law to a Hamiltonian

For \(F=-kx\), the potential is \(V(x)=kx^2/2\). Writing \(\omega=\sqrt{k/m}\) gives

\[\hat H=-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2}{dx^2}+\frac12m\omega^2x^2\]

The potential rises without bound on both sides, so every stationary state is bound.

Natural oscillator scales

Balance kinetic and potential energies to define the oscillator length

\[x_0=\sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{m\omega}},\qquad \xi=\frac{x}{x_0},\qquad \epsilon=\frac{E}{\hbar\omega}\]

\(x_0\) sets the spatial width of low-energy states; \(\hbar\omega\) is the energy spacing scale.

Dimensionless Schrödinger equation

Substitution removes the physical constants from the differential equation:

\[\frac{d^2\psi}{d\xi^2}+(2\epsilon-\xi^2)\psi=0\]

The mass and frequency survive only through the conversion between \(\xi\) and \(x\), and between \(\epsilon\) and \(E\).

Asymptotic clue

For \(|\xi|\gg1\), the \(-\xi^2\psi\) term dominates. The acceptable branch behaves as

\[\psi(\xi)\sim e^{-\xi^2/2}\]

The growing branch \(e^{+\xi^2/2}\) is not normalizable. Factoring out the Gaussian isolates the remaining polynomial structure.

Deriving the oscillator scale

The oscillator length follows from balancing the characteristic kinetic energy \(\hbar^2/(2mx_0^2)\) with the characteristic potential energy \(m\omega^2x_0^2/2\). Equality gives \(x_0^2=\hbar/(m\omega)\). This is not merely a convenient substitution: it estimates the width over which zero-point motion spreads the particle. A larger mass or frequency reduces that width because localization then costs less kinetic energy relative to the steep confinement.

Reading the dimensionless equation

After \(x=x_0\xi\) and \(E=\hbar\omega\epsilon\), every oscillator obeys the same equation. Its only adjustable quantity is the dimensionless eigenvalue \(\epsilon\). This universality means that solving once determines an electron in a molecular vibration and an atom in a trap; their graphs in \(\xi\) are identical, while physical widths and energy spacings differ through \(x_0\) and \(\hbar\omega\).

Why scaling matters

Dimensionless variables reveal the universal problem. Once the equation is solved in \(\xi\), every physical oscillator is obtained by rescaling position with \(x_0\) and energy with \(\hbar\omega\).
