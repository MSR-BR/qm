Delta-function well and scattering
A singular potential replaces derivative continuity with a jump condition
12 / 15
Guided reading

The delta potential is the zero-width idealization of a sharply localized interaction. The wave function remains continuous, but integrating Schrödinger's equation across the singularity reveals a finite discontinuity in its derivative.

Attractive delta well

Let \(V(x)=-g\delta(x)\), with \(g>0\). A bound state has \(E<0\) and

\[\psi(x)=A e^{-\kappa|x|},\qquad \kappa=\frac{\sqrt{-2mE}}{\hbar}\]
Derivative jump
\[\psi'(0^+)-\psi'(0^-)=-\frac{2mg}{\hbar^2}\psi(0)\]

For the decaying ansatz, this yields \(\kappa=mg/\hbar^2\), and therefore

\[E_b=-\frac{mg^2}{2\hbar^2}\]
One and only one bound state

Normalization gives \(A=\sqrt{\kappa}\). The attractive delta well supports exactly one bound state: it is even, nodeless and increasingly localized as \(mg\) grows.

\[\psi_b(x)=\sqrt{\kappa}\,e^{-\kappa|x|}\]
Delta scattering

For \(E=\hbar^2k^2/2m>0\), define \(\alpha=mg/\hbar^2\). Matching \(\psi\) and the derivative jump gives

\[T=\frac{k^2}{k^2+\alpha^2},\qquad R=\frac{\alpha^2}{k^2+\alpha^2}\]

High-energy particles transmit more easily; increasing mass or coupling increases reflection.

Deriving the delta jump

Integrate the stationary Schrödinger equation from \(-\varepsilon\) to \(+\varepsilon\). The energy term vanishes as \(\varepsilon\to0\), while \(\int\delta(x)\psi(x)dx=\psi(0)\). The kinetic term becomes \(-\hbar^2[\psi'(0^+)-\psi'(0^-)]/(2m)\). Equating terms gives the derivative discontinuity. The wave function itself remains continuous; otherwise its second derivative would contain a stronger singularity not present in the potential.

Bound state and scattering share one scale

The parameter \(\alpha=mg/\hbar^2\) has units of inverse length. It is both the bound-state decay constant and the scale that competes with the incident wave number in scattering. Thus the same coupling that creates \(E_b=-\hbar^2\alpha^2/(2m)\) produces \(T=k^2/(k^2+\alpha^2)\). Low-energy waves are strongly reflected, while short-wavelength waves resolve the narrow interaction less strongly.

Do not impose ordinary derivative continuity

A finite step has continuous \(\psi'\), but a delta potential is singular. The correct jump condition comes from integrating the differential equation across an infinitesimal interval.
