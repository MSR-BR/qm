Bound and unbound states: chapter roadmap
Discrete spectra, continuum states and the logic of one-dimensional models
1 / 15
Guided reading

Chapter 4 turns the general rules of wave mechanics into a systematic problem-solving method. The potential divides space into regions; the time-independent Schrödinger equation determines the local form of the wave function; boundary and normalizability conditions select the physically allowed solutions.

Two energy regimes

A bound state is spatially localized and normalizable. Its wave function decays where the energy lies below the asymptotic potential, and its allowed energies form a discrete set.

An unbound state extends to infinity. It represents propagation or scattering and belongs to a continuous energy spectrum.

\[\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|\psi(x)|^2\,dx=1\quad\text{(bound state)}\]
The master equation

Every model begins with

\[-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2\psi}{dx^2}+V(x)\psi=E\psi\]

The sign of \(E-V\) controls the local behavior: oscillatory when \(E>V\), exponential when \(E<V\). This single observation anticipates confinement, penetration and tunneling.

A reusable solve sequence
  • Sketch \(V(x)\), mark the energy, and divide space into regions.
  • Write the correct local solution in each region.
  • Discard divergent terms and impose continuity conditions.
  • Use normalization for bound states or probability current for scattering states.
  • Check dimensions, limiting cases and physical interpretation.

How one equation produces two spectra

A bound-state energy is not known before matching. Choose a trial energy, solve in every region, and ask whether one globally continuous, differentiable and normalizable wave function exists. Usually it does only for isolated energies, creating a discrete spectrum. For an unbound state, any positive incident energy is allowed; matching instead fixes reflected and transmitted amplitudes. The spectrum is continuous, but the coefficients still contain detailed information about the potential.

Local form → global physics

Quantization and scattering are not separate rules. Both follow from joining local solutions into one acceptable global wave function. For a bound state, the matching conditions select isolated energies. For a scattering state, they determine amplitude ratios and therefore \(R\) and \(T\).
