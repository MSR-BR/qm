Scattering from an attractive finite well
Interference inside the well produces resonant transparency
11 / 15
Guided reading

For positive incident energy, an attractive finite well no longer traps the particle; it becomes a scattering region. The wave number is larger inside the well, and multiple internal reflections interfere.

Three-region solution

For a well of width \(L\), \(V=-V_0\) inside and \(V=0\) outside:

\[k=\frac{\sqrt{2mE}}{\hbar},\qquad q=\frac{\sqrt{2m(E+V_0)}}{\hbar}\]
\[\psi_I=Ae^{ikx}+Be^{-ikx},\quad \psi_{II}=Ce^{iqx}+De^{-iqx},\quad \psi_{III}=Fe^{ikx}\]
Exact transmission
\[T=\left[1+\frac{V_0^2}{4E(E+V_0)}\sin^2(qL)\right]^{-1}\]

Even though the potential is attractive, abrupt changes in wavelength cause reflection.

Resonant transparency

Whenever the internal phase is an integer multiple of \(\pi\),

\[qL=n\pi\quad\Longrightarrow\quad T=1\]

The internal round trips interfere destructively in the reflected channel. The well becomes transparent at discrete incident energies.

Useful limits
  • As \(E\gg V_0\), wavelength mismatch becomes small and \(T\to1\).
  • At resonance, \(T=1\) even when the well is deep.
  • Between resonances, \(T\) oscillates with \(E\), \(L\) and \(V_0\).
  • Always verify \(0\le R,T\le1\) and \(R+T=1\).
A phase-matching phenomenon

Transmission resonances are the scattering analogue of standing-wave quantization: both occur when the phase accumulated across a finite region is compatible with boundary matching.
