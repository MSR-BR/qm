Guided reading

This page is a practice map rather than a list of answers. Each problem should begin by identifying the regime and end by checking a physical limit. The middle algebra changes from model to model, but the reasoning pattern is stable.

Model A: finite-well spectrum
  • Compute \(z_0=a\sqrt{2mV_0}/\hbar\).
  • Separate even and odd transcendental equations.
  • Bracket each root between singularities.
  • Convert \(y=\kappa a\) into \(E=-\hbar^2y^2/(2ma^2)\).
  • Check \(-V_0<E<0\) and count nodes.
Model B: QHO moments

Start from \(\hat x=x_0(\hat a+\hat a^\dagger)/\sqrt2\). Terms that change \(n\) cannot contribute to a diagonal expectation value. The surviving products give

\[\langle x^2\rangle_n=x_0^2\left(n+\frac12\right)\]
Model C: transmission
  • Determine whether the interaction region is oscillatory or evanescent.
  • Write incident, reflected and transmitted components with correct directions.
  • Match \(\psi,\psi'\) at finite steps.
  • Form \(T=j_{\rm trans}/j_{\rm inc}\), including velocity factors.
  • Check \(R+T=1\), high-energy and wide-barrier limits.

A complete finite-well calculation

A reliable numerical solution begins by forming the dimensionless depth, locating every even and odd root, and converting roots back to energy. For each root, reconstruct the piecewise wave function, obtain amplitude ratios from continuity, normalize including the tails, and count nodes. The node count orders the states and helps detect a missed root. Finally, compare tail length with \(1/\kappa\) and verify that the state becomes more confined as the well deepens.

A complete scattering calculation

State the incidence direction and eliminate incoming waves from the far right. Use the correct oscillatory or evanescent form in each region, match at every boundary, and calculate currents only after amplitudes are known. Test \(R+T=1\), the high-energy limit, and either the zero-width or wide-barrier limit. These checks are part of the solution, not optional commentary, because they connect the algebra to physical probability conservation.

A disciplined final line

After any numerical or symbolic result, write one sentence explaining what happens when confinement becomes stronger, energy becomes larger, or the barrier becomes wider. That sentence often catches an algebraic error.
