Expectation values and transverse variance
A sharp projection leaves unavoidable transverse quantum spread
9 / 15
Guided reading

In an Lz eigenstate, symmetry makes the mean transverse components vanish, but their squared values do not. Their variance carries the angular momentum not contained in the z projection.

Mean components

Since Lx and Ly are sums of ladder operators, their expectation values connect |l,m⟩ to orthogonal states:

\[\langle L_x\rangle=\langle L_y\rangle=0\]
Equal transverse second moments

Rotational symmetry around z makes the two transverse spreads equal. From \(L_x^2+L_y^2=L^2-L_z^2\),

\[\langle L_x^2\rangle=\langle L_y^2\rangle=\frac{\hbar^2}{2}[l(l+1)-m^2]\]
Standard deviations
\[\Delta L_x=\Delta L_y=\frac{\hbar}{\sqrt2}\sqrt{l(l+1)-m^2}\]
Projection state

Because |l,m⟩ is an Lz eigenstate, \(\Delta L_z=0\). This certainty does not mean the vector is aligned exactly with z; transverse measurements still have distributions.

Extremal states

At m = ±l, the transverse variance is minimized but remains \(\hbar^2l/2\) for each component. The total transverse mean square is \(\hbar^2l\), matching the difference between \(l(l+1)\hbar^2\) and \(l^2\hbar^2\).

Classical analogy and limit

The cone radius resembles a classical transverse component, but quantum variance is not an unknown fixed value. For large l and suitable states, relative fluctuations can become small, creating a semiclassical orientation.

Algebra-to-physics audit

Before accepting an angular-momentum calculation, verify four layers. Operator layer: preserve order and use the correct commutator sign. Spectrum layer: confirm that m changes only in unit steps and remains between -l and +l. Representation layer: state the basis order before reading matrix rows or columns, and check Hermiticity for observables. Physics layer: distinguish an exact eigenvalue from an expectation value or variance. A zero transverse mean does not imply a zero transverse measurement; a basis rotation does not change the underlying observable; and a semiclassical vector cone does not assign simultaneous sharp values to all three components. These checks connect the formal algebra to what an actual sequence of measurements can reveal.

Variance completes the state description

Magnitude and projection give exact eigenvalues; transverse variances quantify what remains uncertain. Together they prevent the misleading picture of a perfectly known classical vector.
