Why matrix mechanics is needed
Discrete quantum systems need a vector language
1 / 15
Guided reading

Chapter 2 built quantum mechanics with wave functions. Chapter 3 keeps the same physical postulates, but rewrites the theory in a form better suited to discrete systems. The guiding example is the Stern-Gerlach experiment: a beam enters an apparatus, and the result is not a continuous smear but a small set of possible outcomes.

Same physics, different representation

Wave mechanics and matrix mechanics are equivalent. The state can be written as a wave function \(\Psi(x,t)\) when position is the convenient basis, or as a vector of coefficients when the system is naturally described by a finite set of alternatives.

\[\Psi(x,t)=\sum_n C_n(t)\psi_n(x) \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad |\Psi(t)\rangle=\sum_n C_n(t)|n\rangle.\]

The right side is not a new theory. It is the same state written in the language of Hilbert-space vectors. This is the language that makes spin, two-level systems and measurement sequences compact.

Why spin is the perfect example

A spin-1/2 degree of freedom has only two outcomes along a chosen axis. This makes the algebra visible: a state is a two-component vector, an observable is a \(2\times 2\) matrix, and a measurement is a projection onto one of two basis vectors.

\[|\Psi\rangle=C_+|+\rangle+C_-|-\rangle,\qquad |C_+|^2+|C_-|^2=1.\]

The Stern-Gerlach apparatus gives the physical interpretation: the coefficients tell how the incoming beam divides into possible outgoing beams.

How to read this chapter

Each page should be read as a translation exercise. A physical operation in the Stern-Gerlach setup becomes a vector operation; a possible spot on the screen becomes an eigenvalue; a blocked beam becomes state preparation.

The central skill is to connect the algebra \(|\Psi\rangle\), \(\hat A\), \(\langle n|\Psi\rangle\) with what the apparatus does to the beam.
