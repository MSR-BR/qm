Hilbert space, bases, superposition and completeness
How a spin state is expanded in different measurement bases
4 / 15
Guided reading

The same incoming beam can be described in different bases. A basis is tied to a measurement question: \(z\)-oriented magnets ask about \(\sigma_z\), while \(x\)-oriented magnets ask about \(\sigma_x\).

Expansion in a basis

In any orthonormal basis \(\{|n\rangle\}\), the state can be expanded as

\[|\Psi\rangle=\sum_n C_n|n\rangle,\qquad C_n=\langle n|\Psi\rangle.\]

The coefficient is a projection amplitude. In wave mechanics the same idea appears when a wave function is expanded in eigenfunctions.

Completeness

A complete basis resolves the identity operator:

\[\sum_n |n\rangle\langle n|=\hat 1.\]

Inserting this identity into a state gives the expansion above. This is not a trick; it says the chosen measurement basis is enough to reconstruct the state.

Spin bases along z and x

For spin-1/2, the \(z\)-basis and the \(x\)-basis are related by superposition:

\[|+\rangle_x=\frac{|+\rangle+|-\rangle}{\sqrt2},\qquad |-\rangle_x=\frac{|+\rangle-|-\rangle}{\sqrt2}.\]

This single relation explains a major Stern-Gerlach observation: a beam prepared as \(|+\rangle\) along \(z\) splits into two equal parts when measured along \(x\).

Conceptual point

Superposition does not mean the atom secretly took two paths in a classical sense. It means that a state sharp for one measurement can be a linear combination of possible outcomes for another measurement.

Changing the magnet direction changes the basis in which the same vector is decomposed.
