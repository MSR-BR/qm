Dirac notation: kets, bras and inner products
The state postulate in vector form
3 / 15
Guided reading

Dirac notation gives a compact way to write the same ideas that appeared in wave mechanics. The ket is the state; the bra is its dual; the inner product gives overlaps, normalization and eventually measurement probabilities.

Postulate for quantum states

A physical state is represented by a vector in Hilbert space. In Dirac notation this vector is written as a ket, \(|\Psi\rangle\). For the Stern-Gerlach spin example, the natural basis along \(z\) is made of two states:

\[|+\rangle=\begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix},\qquad |-\rangle=\begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}.\]

These are not positions in ordinary space. They are basis vectors in the abstract space of possible spin states.

Bras and inner products

The bra \(\langle\Psi|\) is the dual of the ket. If \(|\Psi\rangle\) is a column vector, \(\langle\Psi|\) is the conjugate-transposed row vector.

\[|\Psi\rangle=\begin{pmatrix}C_+\\C_-\end{pmatrix}\quad \Rightarrow \quad \langle\Psi|=\begin{pmatrix}C_+^*&C_-^*\end{pmatrix}.\]

The inner product \(\langle\Phi|\Psi\rangle\) measures the overlap between two states. Orthogonal Stern-Gerlach outcomes have zero overlap.

Normalization and orthogonality

Because probabilities must add to one, the state vector must be normalized:

\[\langle\Psi|\Psi\rangle=1.\]

For the \(z\)-basis of spin, the basis vectors satisfy

\[\langle+|+\rangle=1,\quad \langle-| -\rangle=1,\quad \langle+|-\rangle=0.\]

That is the vector version of a clean experimental distinction: the upper and lower beams are mutually exclusive outcomes of the same measurement.

Physical meaning

In Stern-Gerlach language, \(\langle+|\Psi\rangle\) is the amplitude for the incoming state to be found in the upper \(z\)-beam. The probability will be its squared modulus, but the amplitude itself can be complex. This is why the vector notation must keep phases, not just intensities.

The algebra stores more information than a probability table: it stores the phases that matter when a later measurement uses another basis.
