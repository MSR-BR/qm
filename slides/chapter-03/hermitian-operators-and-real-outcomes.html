Hermitian operators and real outcomes
Why observables are represented by self-adjoint matrices
10 / 15
Guided reading

Observables must give real measurement results. Hermitian operators are the mathematical class that guarantees this property and provides orthogonal eigenstates for distinct outcomes.

Dagger operation

For a matrix operator, the adjoint is the transpose plus complex conjugation:

\[\hat A^\dagger=(\hat A^*)^T.\]

An operator is Hermitian when it equals its own adjoint:

\[\hat A^\dagger=\hat A.\]

The Pauli matrices used for Stern-Gerlach spin components satisfy this condition.

Real eigenvalues

Suppose \(\hat A|a\rangle=a|a\rangle\) and \(\hat A\) is Hermitian. Then

\[\langle a|\hat A|a\rangle=a\langle a|a\rangle,\]

but Hermiticity also implies that this expectation value equals its own complex conjugate. For a normalized eigenstate,

\[a=a^*.\]

Thus the eigenvalue is real, as a measurement result must be.

Real expectation values

For any normalized state, the expectation value of a Hermitian operator is real:

\[\langle \hat A\rangle^*=\left(\langle\Psi|\hat A|\Psi\rangle\right)^*=\langle\Psi|\hat A^\dagger|\Psi\rangle=\langle\hat A\rangle.\]

This is why the postulate for observables requires Hermitian operators rather than arbitrary matrices.

Back to Stern-Gerlach

The outcomes \(+1\) and \(-1\) of \(\hat\sigma_z\) or \(\hat\sigma_x\) are real eigenvalues. The apparatus can display them as separate beams because the corresponding eigenstates are orthogonal.

Hermiticity is the algebraic reason that a measurement has real, distinguishable outcomes.
