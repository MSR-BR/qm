Unitary transformations and change of basis
Changing coordinates without changing physics
14 / 15
Guided reading

A basis change is a change in representation, not a change in the physical state. Unitary transformations are the transformations that preserve inner products, normalization and probabilities.

Unitary condition

A transformation \(\hat U\) is unitary when

\[\hat U^\dagger\hat U=\hat U\hat U^\dagger=\hat 1.\]

This condition preserves lengths and overlaps:

\[\langle\Phi'|\Psi'\rangle=\langle\Phi|\Psi\rangle.\]

Because probabilities are squared overlaps, a unitary change of basis cannot change predictions.

States and operators

Under a change of basis, the state components and operator matrix both transform:

\[|\Psi'\rangle=\hat U^\dagger|\Psi\rangle,\qquad \hat A'=\hat U^\dagger\hat A\hat U.\]

The expectation value is invariant:

\[\langle\Psi'|\hat A'|\Psi'\rangle=\langle\Psi|\hat A|\Psi\rangle.\]
z basis and x basis

For spin-1/2, the transformation from the \(z\)-basis to the \(x\)-basis can be represented by

\[U=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}.\]

This matrix contains the same information as

\[|+\rangle_x=\frac{|+\rangle+|-\rangle}{\sqrt2},\qquad |-\rangle_x=\frac{|+\rangle-|-\rangle}{\sqrt2}.\]

Thus the algebraic change of basis is exactly the physical change from SG(Z) to SG(X).

What stays fixed

The numbers displayed by a correct calculation are basis-independent. What changes is the coordinate description used to compute them.
