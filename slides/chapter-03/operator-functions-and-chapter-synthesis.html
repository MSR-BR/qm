Matrix exponentials and the bridge back to wave mechanics

Guided reading

The final technical tool is the idea of applying functions to operators. This is needed for time evolution, rotations and many later calculations. The chapter then closes the loop: matrix mechanics is wave mechanics written in another basis.

Functions of operators

If \(\hat A\) has spectral decomposition \(\hat A=\sum_n a_n|a_n\rangle\langle a_n|\), then a function of \(\hat A\) is defined by applying the function to its eigenvalues:

\[f(\hat A)=\sum_n f(a_n)|a_n\rangle\langle a_n|.\]

This definition is what makes expressions such as \(e^{-i\hat Ht/\hbar}\) precise.

Pauli exponential

Because \(\hat\sigma_x^2=\hat 1\), the exponential separates into even and odd powers:

\[e^{\alpha\hat\sigma_x}=\cosh(\alpha)\hat 1+\sinh(\alpha)\hat\sigma_x.\]

With \(\alpha=-i\theta\), this becomes the familiar unitary form

\[e^{-i\theta\hat\sigma_x}=\cos\theta\,\hat 1-i\sin\theta\,\hat\sigma_x.\]

Chapter 3 in one sentence

Matrix mechanics says: represent the state by a vector, represent measurable questions by Hermitian operators, use projections for probabilities, and use unitary transformations for time evolution and basis changes.

The Stern-Gerlach experiment is the running laboratory where each of these rules becomes visible.
