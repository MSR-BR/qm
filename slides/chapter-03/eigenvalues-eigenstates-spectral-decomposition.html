Eigenvalues, eigenstates and spectral decomposition
Possible results are encoded in an operator eigenbasis
6 / 15
Guided reading

This section slows down the meaning of the eigenvalue equation. In matrix mechanics, measurement is not introduced after the algebra; it is already encoded in the operator. The eigenvectors are the states that pass through the apparatus with a definite result, and the eigenvalues are the possible numbers associated with those results.

Definite-value states

The eigenvalue equation is

\[\hat A|a_n\rangle=a_n|a_n\rangle.\]

If the incoming state is \(|a_n\rangle\), the measurement of \(\hat A\) gives \(a_n\) with certainty. That is the algebraic version of a beam that exits through only one channel of the apparatus.

The chapter assumes non-degenerate examples at this stage. That means each observed result corresponds to a single state in the simplified discussion. Degeneracy will require more care in later chapters.

Spectral decomposition

When the eigenstates form a complete orthonormal basis, the operator can be written as a sum over eigenvalues multiplied by projectors:

\[\hat A=\sum_n a_n|a_n\rangle\langle a_n|.\]

This formula is one of the most useful in the chapter. It separates the measurement into two ingredients: the value \(a_n\) and the projector \(|a_n\rangle\langle a_n|\) that extracts the corresponding component of the state.

Projectors as experimental filters

A projector behaves like an idealized filter. Acting on a state, it keeps only the part aligned with the selected eigenstate:

\[\hat P_n|\Psi\rangle=|a_n\rangle\langle a_n|\Psi\rangle.\]

In a Stern-Gerlach sequence, blocking one beam is the laboratory version of applying a projector and then renormalizing the surviving state.

Two-level Stern-Gerlach case

For a spin projection along an axis \(u\), the dimensionless observable has two eigenvalues:

\[\hat\sigma_u|+\rangle_u=+|+\rangle_u,\\qquad \hat\sigma_u|-\rangle_u=-|-\rangle_u.\]

The two output beams are not arbitrary branches. They correspond to the two eigenstates of the operator selected by the magnet orientation.

Why observables must be Hermitian

The book points out that physical observables must be represented by operators with real eigenvalues. Hermitian operators supply this property. Thus the operator postulate is not merely formal: it guarantees that measured quantities can be represented by real outcomes.

\[\hat A^\dagger=\hat A \quad \Rightarrow \quad a_n\in\mathbb R.\]

The Pauli matrices are the first concrete examples of such operators in this chapter.

What this prepares

Once spectral decomposition is understood, the derivations of probability and expectation value become natural. The Born rule uses the projector amplitude \(\langle a_n|\Psi\rangle\), and the expectation value uses the weighted sum of eigenvalues.
