Eigenvalues, eigenstates and spectral decomposition
Possible results are encoded in an operator eigenbasis
6 / 15
Guided reading

The eigenvalue equation is where the mathematical object becomes a measurement rule. Eigenvalues are possible results; eigenstates are states that give those results with certainty.

Eigenvalue equation

For an observable \(\hat A\), the eigenvalue equation is

\[\hat A|a_n\rangle=a_n|a_n\rangle.\]

If the state entering the apparatus is \(|a_n\rangle\), the result \(a_n\) is certain. Stern-Gerlach makes this concrete: \(|+\rangle\) entering an SG(Z) device exits only in the upper \(z\)-beam.

Spectral decomposition

For a non-degenerate observable with orthonormal eigenstates, the operator can be rebuilt from its eigenvalues and projectors:

\[\hat A=\sum_n a_n |a_n\rangle\langle a_n|.\]

This form is powerful because it separates the two ingredients of a measurement: the values \(a_n\) and the projectors \(|a_n\rangle\langle a_n|\) that select the corresponding component of the state.

Projectors as beam selectors

The projector onto one outcome is

\[\hat P_n=|a_n\rangle\langle a_n|.\]

Physically, it behaves like an ideal filter: it keeps the component of the state aligned with \(|a_n\rangle\) and removes the orthogonal components. This is exactly how the book uses blocked Stern-Gerlach beams to discuss preparation.

Why Hermitian operators enter

A measurement result must be real, and different sharp outcomes must be distinguishable. Hermitian operators supply both properties: real eigenvalues and orthogonal eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues.
